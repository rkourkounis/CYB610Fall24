General Information

Data breach notification laws in the United States are designed to protect individualsâ€™ personal information by requiring entities to notify affected parties in the event of a data breach. At the federal level, laws such as the Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act (HIPAA) and the Gramm-Leach-Bliley Act (GLBA) mandate notifications for breaches involving protected health information and non-public personal information, respectively. Additionally, the Computer-Security Incident Notification Requirements for Banking Organizations require FDIC-supervised banking organizations to notify the FDIC within 36 hours of a significant computer security incident.

All 50 states, along with the District of Columbia, Guam, Puerto Rico, and the U.S. Virgin Islands, have enacted their own data breach notification laws. These state laws generally require entities that own, control, or process personal data to notify affected individuals and, in some cases, state authorities, when a data breach occurs. Key elements of these state laws include specific notification timeframes, requirements for notifying state agencies or consumer protection agencies, and provisions for substitute notice if direct notification is not feasible. Some states also provide individuals with a private right of action to sue for damages resulting from a data breach.

Given the absence of a comprehensive federal data breach notification law, compliance with state-specific laws is crucial for entities handling personal data. Organizations must assess whether they own, control, or license personally identifiable information to determine if a breach has occurred and follow the relevant notification requirements. Resources like the State Data Breach Notification Chart and overviews of U.S. data breach notification laws can provide valuable guidance for navigating these regulations.
